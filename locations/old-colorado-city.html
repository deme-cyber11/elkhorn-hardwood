Old Colorado City's Historic Character Homes

Old Colorado City (OCC) predates Colorado Springs itself, founded in 1859 as the original settlement at the base of Ute Pass. Today this vibrant arts district along West Colorado Avenue is a treasured neighborhood of Victorian cottages, Craftsman bungalows, and early 20th-century character homes â€” many with original hardwood floors that have survived over a century of Colorado life.

These aren't ordinary floors. OCC homes frequently feature longleaf pine (a wood so dense it's no longer commercially available), narrow-strip red oak with tight grain patterns, and Douglas fir planks with a warmth and character that modern milled lumber simply cannot replicate. Refinishing these floors requires a craftsman's touch â€” knowledge of historic wood species, access to salvage materials for repairs, and the patience to sand carefully without removing more board thickness than necessary.

Common Flooring Challenges in OCC Homes

  • Multiple layers of old finish: Many OCC floors have been refinished 2â€“4 times over the decades, sometimes with incompatible products layered on top of each other. Proper sanding removes all previous coats to reach clean bare wood.
  • Board damage and gaps: A century of Colorado's dry winters means seasonal contraction has widened gaps between boards. We use flexible fillers that expand and contract with the wood rather than rigid putty that cracks and falls out.
  • Irreplaceable wood species: Longleaf pine and old-growth Douglas fir cannot be purchased new. When boards need replacement, we source from our salvage wood inventory of reclaimed hardwood matched by species, grain, and width.
  • Thin remaining boards: After multiple refinishings, some boards may be too thin for aggressive sanding. We assess thickness with a gauge and adjust our sanding approach â€” sometimes screening (light abrasion) is the right call instead of a full sand.
